## Music: 85 Posts

The first music post appeared July 23, 2008: "Michael Brecker," a brief tribute to the jazz saxophonist. It's not mathematical at all‚Äîjust an appreciation.

But by October 2009, music had become a mathematical topic: "Circle of fifths and number theory." The question is concrete: why can you cycle through all twelve notes of the chromatic scale by going up by fifths, but not by thirds or sixths? The answer involves modular arithmetic and the fact that 7 (the number of semitones in a fifth) is coprime to 12.

This is the pattern throughout. Music posts begin with a musical observation and arrive at mathematics:

- "Logarithms, music, and arsenic" (2008): Why musical intervals are measured in logarithms
- "Mathematics and piano tuning" (2022): The compromises of equal temperament
- "How many musical scales are there?" (2017): Counting with combinatorics
- "The acoustics of kettledrums" (2016): Bessel functions in drumheads
- "Dividing an octave into 14 pieces" (2022): Alternative temperaments

Nineteen posts carry both the Math and Music categories. These are the intersection points, where the two interests don't just coexist but merge:

- "Circle of fifths and roots of two" (2009)
- "Perfect fifths, octaves, and an ergodic map" (2021)
- "Overtones and Barbershop Quartets" (2025)
- "Equipentatonic scale" (2025)

The mathematical content is real. Bessel functions describe vibrating circular membranes (drumheads). The overtone series is a Fourier decomposition. Equal temperament is a compromise between irrational and rational numbers‚Äîyou can't have both perfect fifths and perfect octaves using the same tuning system.

Music appears because it can't not appear. The mathematics of sound, scale, and rhythm is too rich to ignore. A mathematician who plays or listens to music will eventually notice the structure.

## Unicode: 72 Posts

An unexpected obsession.

72 posts tag Unicode‚Äîmore than Music or Typography. And the posts span the entire blog:

## What the Edges Reveal

The edge categories share a pattern: they're not mathematics, but they involve mathematics.

Music involves frequencies, ratios, combinatorics. Typography involves encoding, rendering, visual precision. Clinical trials involve experimental design, Bayesian statistics, optimization. Unicode involves discrete structures, internationalization, edge cases.

The blog doesn't have a category for "pure hobbies"‚Äîno posts about sports, cooking, or travel as ends in themselves. The edges are edges of the mathematical map, not departures from it. They're places where mathematical thinking applies to non-mathematical domains.

This suggests something about the author's intellectual style: everything becomes a math problem. Hear a piece of music? Wonder about the frequency ratios. Encounter a character encoding issue? Investigate the standard. Design a clinical trial? Apply optimal stopping theory.

The edges aren't distractions from the center. They're extensions of it.

## The Clinical Trial Mystery

One edge is different: clinical trials.

Music persists for seventeen years. Typography persists. Unicode intensifies. But clinical trials end‚Äîcleanly, completely, in 2018.

The blog doesn't explain the break. There's no farewell post, no "I'm moving on from clinical work." The topic simply stops appearing. The silence is more eloquent than any explanation.

Career transitions leave traces in the data. The clinical trials arc traces a professional identity: someone working in pharmaceutical statistics from 2008 through 2018, then not. The work continues elsewhere, presumably, but not on the blog.

This is what edges can reveal: not just interests but their boundaries. Music has no boundary‚Äîit runs from 2008 to 2025. Clinical trials have a sharp one. The difference tells a story about which interests are personal (music) and which were professional (clinical work).

## Humanization Through Data

The edges humanize the center.

A blog that was purely mathematical would be abstract, impersonal, a textbook in slow motion. The edges reveal a person: someone who listens to music, cares about fonts, worked in clinical statistics, and notices the details of character encoding.

The outline posed a question: "What does a mathematician do when he's not doing math?"

The answer from the data: he finds math in everything else. The edges of the map are not departures from mathematics but applications of it to domains mathematics doesn't usually claim. Music, typography, geography, encoding‚Äîall become mathematical when a mathematical mind engages with them.

The blog's edges are not escapes from the center. They're expeditions from it‚Äîand they always return.
